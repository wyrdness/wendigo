## Overview

The wendigo is a malevolent spirit-being from the traditions of Algonquian-speaking peoples of northern North America, including the Ojibwe (Anishinaabe), Cree, Innu (Naskapi-Montagnais), Algonquin, and Saulteaux. Spellings vary widely across languages and orthographies: Anishinaabemowin wiindigoo, Cree wīhtikōw / wetiko / witiko, and Innu atchen / atshen. Traditional teachings describe the wendigo as gaunt, skeletal, ash-gray, and associated with winter, ice, hunger, and isolation; some teachings cast it as a giant many times human size that grows larger with every victim consumed and yet remains eternally starving. The wendigo is most strongly associated with the gravest of social taboos — cannibalism — and tradition holds that humans may become wendigos through resorting to cannibalism in famine, through sustained contact with a wendigo, or through being overcome by greed. The word's English-language popularisation owes much to Algernon Blackwood's 1910 novella The Wendigo. Within Algonquian communities the wendigo continues to be invoked as a moral and ecological figure — increasingly read by Indigenous scholars (e.g., Jack D. Forbes, Robin Wall Kimmerer, Basil Johnston) as a metaphor for greed, ecological destruction, and colonial violence — quite distinct from the wendigo of horror fiction.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: From Proto-Algonquian *wi·nteko·wa, possibly meaning 'owl' or 'malevolent spirit'; reflexes include Ojibwe wiindigoo, Cree wīhtikōw, and Innu atchen.
- **Meaning**: Often glossed in English as 'cannibal spirit' or 'evil being of winter'.
- **First use**: Algonquian languages prior to European contact; English-language attestations from the 17th century onwards (e.g., in Jesuit Relations); the spelling 'wendigo' popularised by Blackwood (1910).

## Notable Sightings

### Swift Runner (Ka-Ki-Si-Kutchin) wendigo case ⭐
> **Date**: 1878-12-01  
> **Location**: Near Fort Saskatchewan, North-West Territories (now Alberta), Canada  
> **Witnesses**: 1 (Defendant's confession, North-West Mounted Police investigation)  
Plains Cree man Swift Runner killed and cannibalised his wife, mother, brother, and six children in the winter of 1878–1879, despite sufficient food being available at a nearby Hudson's Bay Company post. He confessed and reportedly described his condition in terms of being possessed by a wendigo. He was tried and hanged at Fort Saskatchewan on December 20, 1879 — the first judicial execution in what is now Alberta.
**Credibility**: high — Frequently cited in the wendigo-psychosis debate as the strongest single behavioural case.
**Sources**: Carlson, N. (2009), 'Reviving Witiko (Windigo)', Ethnohistory 56(3)

### Jack Fiddler / Joseph Fiddler wendigo arrests ⭐
> **Date**: 1907-09-30  
> **Location**: Sandy Lake / Norway House region, northwestern Ontario / Manitoba  
> **Witnesses**: 0 (North-West Mounted Police, Sucker clan community)  
> **Evidence**: Royal Canadian Mounted Police court records; Sucker-clan oral history (Thomas Fiddler & James Stevens)  
Oji-Cree leaders Jack Fiddler (Zhauwuno-geezhigo-gaubow) and his brother Joseph Fiddler were arrested by the North-West Mounted Police on charges relating to the death of Wahsakapeequay, said within their community to have been turning into a wendigo. Jack Fiddler died by suicide in custody on September 30, 1907; Joseph was convicted of murder and died in prison before a release order arrived.
**Credibility**: high — Marks the imposition of Canadian colonial law over Sucker-clan traditional practice on the wendigo.
**Sources**: Fiddler, T. & Stevens, J. R. (1985), Killing the Shamen

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Real Algonquian spirit-being** _(Algonquian community knowledge-keepers (e.g. Basil Johnston))_

Within many Algonquian community teachings the wendigo is a real spirit-being and not merely a metaphor; engagement with the wendigo is part of a coherent moral and cosmological order.

### Scientific

**Wendigo psychosis as culture-bound syndrome (challenged)** _(Marano, L. (skeptical), Brightman, R. A. (defending material reality), Carlson, N. (2009))_

Earlier 20th-century anthropology and psychiatry described 'wendigo psychosis' as a culture-bound syndrome involving cannibalistic compulsion. Lou Marano (1982) argued that this construct was an emic–etic confusion with little behavioural reality; Robert Brightman (1988) and later ethnohistorians (Carlson 2009) responded with documentary evidence for substantive witiko cases.

**Famine, isolation, and starvation pathology** _(Various medical anthropologists and historians)_

Mainstream medical-anthropological readings interpret historical 'wendigo' incidents as the social interpretation of starvation cannibalism, severe psychiatric disorder, or both, in extreme boreal-forest winters.

### Skeptical

**Cultural-symbolic reading** _(Jack D. Forbes (Powhatan-Renapé / Lenape), Robin Wall Kimmerer (Citizen Potawatomi))_

Some Indigenous and non-Indigenous scholars (e.g. Jack D. Forbes, Robin Wall Kimmerer) read the wendigo primarily as a moral and ecological symbol of unrestrained greed and consumption — a reading that does not require a literal external entity.


## Indigenous Perspectives

> ⚠️ **The wendigo is sacred or at least tradition-internal material in many Algonquian communities. Indigenous scholars (e.g. Basil Johnston, Robin Wall Kimmerer, Brady DeSanti) have repeatedly criticised the use of the wendigo in non-Indigenous horror fiction as decontextualising and harmful.**

**Cultures**: Anishinaabe (Ojibwe), Cree (Plains, Woodland, Swampy), Innu (Naskapi-Montagnais), Algonquin, Saulteaux, Oji-Cree

An integral feature of Algonquian moral, ecological, and spiritual thought; not reducible to a horror trope.
